"God has attached me to the city like a gadfly to a great and noble horse, which is sluggish due to its size and needs to be stung into life."

Socrates argues that his annoying questioning is a divine service to keep Athens alert and virtuous. Killing him would be harming themselves.

III. The Phaedo

"The Immortality of the Soul"

The final hours in the prison cell. Socrates comforts his weeping friends by proving that for a philosopher, death is not an end, but a liberation.

The Argument from Recollection

We have concepts of perfect Equality, Beauty, and Goodness. We never see perfection in this world. Therefore, we must have known these standards before we were born.

"The soul must have existed before the body to possess this knowledge."

Simmias: "Is the Soul a Harmony?"

Simmias argues that the soul is like the harmony of a lyre. If the lyre (body) breaks, the harmony (soul) vanishes.

Socrates: A harmony cannot direct the instrument; it is a result. But the Soul directs the body (controlling impulses). Therefore, it is not a harmony.

Cebes: "Does the Weaver outlive the cloak?"

Cebes argues the soul might outlive many bodies (reincarnation) but eventually wear out and die, just as a weaver outlives many coats but eventually dies.

Socrates: The Soul is the carrier of Life. Just as Snow cannot admit Fire, the Soul cannot admit Death. It is essentially deathless.

Sunset

The sacred ship has returned. The jailer enters, weeping. Socrates bathes to spare the women the trouble of washing his corpse.

The Hemlock

He takes the cup cheerfully. He prays for a prosperous journey. He drinks it in one breath.

The Final Words

"Crito, we owe a cock to Asclepius. Pay it and do not neglect it."

Death is the cure for the sickness of life.

"Such was the end of our friend, the wisest and justest and best of all men."
